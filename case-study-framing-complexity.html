Framing complexity

Most teams rush to solutions. I embedded Problem Framing as a discipline across the design org — so we spend our thinking where it actually changes the outcome: on understanding the problem.

My role
Design leader & practice owner
Scope
Org-wide design practice
Inspiration
Design Sprint Academy
Format
Workshops, templates, coaching
Remote Problem Framing workshop · with the Design Sprint Academy
01 The Context

Not every problem deserves the same response — but we were treating them as if they did.

Problem-solving is the daily work of a design team. The trap is that not all problems are the same shape — and the methods that crack an obvious problem fall apart on a genuinely complex one. I wanted the org to be able to tell the difference before committing effort.

Type 01
Obvious
Clear cause and effect. The solution is self-evident — just do it, well.
Type 02
Complicated
Many interconnected parts. Solvable with expertise and a systematic approach.
Type 03
Complex
Unknown causes and effects, unpredictable. Demands experimentation and deep understanding — not a playbook.
“If I had an hour to solve a problem, I’d spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and 5 minutes thinking about solutions.”
— Albert Einstein
02 The Approach

Problem Framing isn’t a way to solve the problem. It makes sure you’re solving the right one.

Problem Framing is a structured workshop that helps a team understand, define and prioritise a complex business problem using data and customer insight. It’s a lens — a way to break a daunting challenge into manageable components and locate the root cause, not the symptom.

It’s usually skipped. Teams feel the urgency to ship, or assume the problem is already understood, and jump straight to solutions. That’s how you get well-built answers to the wrong question — and wasted effort. So I positioned framing firmly in the discovery space, before any solution work begins.

01
Made framing a prerequisite, not an optional
The classic Design Sprint spends ~80% of its time in the solution space. I adopted the Design Sprint 3.0 model, which front-loads Problem Framing — gathering quantitative and qualitative data, interviewing stakeholders, and aligning on a single core problem before the team touches a solution.
02
Brought in outside expertise to set the bar
Rather than invent it internally, I drew on the Design Sprint Academy and ran remote framing workshops with them — borrowing a proven method so the practice started credible, not homegrown and shaky.
03
Built it into tools the team actually uses
We created Miro templates for both mini and in-depth sessions, so the practice flexed to the data available — a fifteen-minute reframe or a full-day workshop ran from the same shared scaffold.
04
Taught it sideways, through the senior designers
I had lead and senior designers educate their peers rather than mandating it top-down. A practice spread by the people who use it sticks; a process imposed by a manager doesn’t.
Miro templates for both mini and in-depth Problem Framing sessions
03 Who Frames

Framing only works with the right people in the room.

A framing session isn’t a designer activity — it’s a cross-functional one. Getting the composition right was half my job:

Decision-makers
Business leaders with the breadth to keep the problem aligned to organisational goals.
Experts
Deep domain knowledge — the people who help surface the real root causes.
Frontline
Those living with the problem daily: engineers, designers, product owners.
Facilitator
A skilled guide who keeps the discussion focused and productive — often me, often a senior I’d coached.
04 The Outcome

A design org that now understands before it builds.

Embedding Problem Framing changed the default behaviour of the team. Complex problems are now interrogated before they’re solved — the work that follows is more confident, more structured, and aimed at the issue that actually matters. The way I got there is reusable for any practice change:

01
Test & refine on small projects first
Prove the approach somewhere low-stakes before asking the org to adopt it.
02
Build alliances beyond design
Win over product leads and business analysts — the practice needs partners outside UX to survive.
03
Share the success stories
Turn early wins into case studies that make the impact of framing undeniable to the next team.
