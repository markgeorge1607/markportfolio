01 The Challenge

A retention problem that needed a structural intervention, not a better UI.

Users treated Just Eat as an occasional treat. Order frequency was inconsistent, re-acquisition costs were rising, and competitors with paid memberships were winning. Paid members at rival platforms were spending 24× more and ordering 10–30% more — and 13% of lapsed Just Eat customers had left specifically for a better loyalty programme.

This wasn't a UX problem. It was a business model problem that needed a design-led strategic intervention: reframe the user relationship from transactional to membership-based, in three months, across every surface.

Tension 01
Time Horizon
Customers want instant gratification. The business needed medium-to-long-term behavioural change. Both had to be true at once.
Tension 02
Competing Metrics
Retention and order frequency are related but distinct. Optimising for one could undermine the other — the team had to hold both.
Tension 03
Value Exchange
Membership had to feel genuinely valuable to users, while remaining commercially sustainable. Neither could be sacrificed.

02 Leading the Team

Three designers. Five squads. One experience that couldn't fragment.

Loyalty touches every part of the product — search, menu, basket, checkout, accounts. My three designers were each embedded in different squads, with different PMs, engineers, and release cycles. The risk wasn't bad design. The risk was incoherent design — individually sensible decisions that added up to a fractured experience.

As manager

I introduced a fortnightly design sync with a fixed agenda: decisions made, decisions pending, one cross-squad dependency to resolve. Not a status meeting — an alignment mechanism. I also created a shared Figma workspace so all three designers' live work was visible in one place. Not for surveillance — for context. When designer A's checkout flow and designer B's account page needed to connect, they could see each other's work without booking a meeting.

03 The Solution

A 90-day pass — not a subscription trap.

Research surfaced subscription fatigue. Users didn't object to paying for value — they objected to being locked in. A flexible, one-time-payment 90-day pass made entry feel risk-free, while the bundle rewarded commitment with real savings. By embedding it as a free-trial upsell directly in the checkout flow, we captured users at peak engagement — already in a spending mindset, already committed to an order.

The strategic call

The original brief asked for a standard subscription. I challenged it. I tasked one designer with a focused competitive audit and concept testing against three models — subscription, bundle, free trial — before we committed. The data came back clearly in favour of the bundle. Making space for that discovery cost two weeks but saved us from building the wrong product. That's a management decision, not a design one.

04 The Amazon & Lieferando Partnership

Two global brands. Three markets. One activation moment.

I led the design and cross-platform integration of the Amazon Prime × Just Eat Takeaway partnership — free delivery for Prime members in Germany, Austria, and Spain. Two brands with different design systems, technical constraints, and definitions of "done." Amazon's single-URL login architecture conflicted with Just Eat's per-market URLs; I made the call to accept the extra country-selection step rather than compromise the security model, and communicated that trade-off clearly to both sides.

Protecting the team

My designers were under pressure from both sides — Amazon's PMs wanted faster turnaround; internal stakeholders wanted more polish. I kept that pressure at my level. I ran weekly stakeholder syncs with Amazon myself, bringing decisions to those calls with rationale and options — so my designers weren't asked to justify their work in rooms they hadn't been part of.

05 Led by Experimentation

Moving the team from "launch and see" to hypothesis-first.

One of my goals was to shift the team's operating model. Every initiative needed a stated hypothesis and a defined success metric before work began. Initially it felt like extra process. Within two months it was how they thought. One early experiment showed £10 credit driving 3.83% redemption vs. 1.49% for unlimited free delivery — at a lower cost per outcome. That data shaped the entire next phase of the programme.

Building the culture

I introduced a hypothesis-first brief template — no work started without a stated hypothesis and a defined success metric. The change wasn't procedural — it was conceptual. Designers who think in hypotheses write better briefs, push back on vague requests more confidently, and advocate for their work in commercial language. That's a career-level shift, not just a process improvement.
