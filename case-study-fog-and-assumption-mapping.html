Facts over guesses

Teams ship on assumptions dressed up as facts. I gave the org two methods — the FOG Method and Assumption Mapping — to separate what we know from what we’re hoping, and test the riskiest bets first.

My role
Design leader & practice owner
Scope
Org-wide design practice
Methods
FOG · Assumption Mapping
Format
Templates, workshops, coaching
Categorising insight — facts, opinions, guesses
01 The Context

The most expensive mistakes start when a guess gets treated like a fact.

Design runs on insight — but insight arrives mixed. Observed user behaviour sits in the same sentence as a stakeholder’s hunch and an unspoken assumption, and the team can’t always tell which is which. When opinions and guesses get acted on as if they were facts, you build the wrong thing confidently — and pay for it in costly revisions later.

I wanted a shared, low-ceremony way for the team to label their certainty, and a way to decide what to validate first. Two complementary methods did the job.

02 Method One — FOG

Sort every insight into Facts, Opinions, Guesses — then earn your way to certainty.

F
Facts
Verifiable — observed in analytics, or confirmed requirements. Safe to build on.
O
Opinions
Informed by experience or intuition, but not backed by data. Useful, not load-bearing.
G
Guesses
Speculative and unverified — the highest risk, and the first thing to test.
01
Gather data from everywhere
Research, analytics, stakeholder interviews. The richer the input, the better the sorting that follows — so we cast wide before we categorised.
02
Categorise honestly into F, O, G
The discipline is admitting when something is an opinion or a guess rather than a fact. Naming the uncertainty out loud is most of the value.
03
Prioritise validating the guesses
Guesses carry the most risk, so they get tested first — user testing, surveys, A/B — converting each guess into a fact, or disproving it cleanly.
04
Re-sort as you learn
New evidence re-categorises old insight, and the design evolves with it — a living board, not a one-off audit.
“You don’t need more opinions in the room. You need to know which ones are facts.”
Why FOG stuck
03 Method Two — Assumption Mapping

Then map the guesses by impact and evidence — and test the dangerous corner.

FOG surfaces the guesses; Assumption Mapping decides which ones are worth your time. We brainstorm every assumption about a product’s desirability, feasibility and viability, then plot them on a 2×2 — evidence on one axis, impact on the other.

The top-left corner does the work: high impact, low evidence — the assumptions that could sink the project and that we know the least about. That’s where testing goes first.

01
Identify the assumptions
Brainstorm what the idea quietly depends on — e.g. for a dog-walking app, that owners will trust a stranger with their pet, or that the demand is even there.
02
Map onto the 2×2
Place each assumption by how much it matters and how much evidence backs it — making the risky, unproven ones impossible to ignore.
03
Test the high-risk corner
Design fast experiments — interviews, surveys, prototypes — for the high-impact, low-evidence assumptions, gathering proof quickly and cheaply.
04
Iterate & refine
Evidence either firms an assumption up or kills it. The map keeps shrinking the unknowns until the product lines up with real user and market reality.
04 The Outcome

Two methods, one habit: decisions grounded in reality, not conjecture.

Used together, the pair is more than the sum: FOG categorises and validates every insight, Assumption Mapping prioritises and tests the most critical unknowns. The team still designs creatively — but on a foundation it has actually checked, which is what keeps costly late revisions off the roadmap.
